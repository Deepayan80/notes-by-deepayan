I’m Reading the Economic Survey So You Don’t Have To: Part 1

Feb 1, 2026 · 3 min read

Hey everyone! I’ve decided to do something a bit "brave" this week—I’ve started reading the official Economic Survey 2025-26.

I know, I know. It sounds like a snooze-fest. But honestly? Once you get past the charts and the jargon, it’s a fascinating look at where the data says our country is heading. Think of it as India's official "Year in Review" and "Future Roadmap" rolled into one.

I’m breaking my notes down into a series of posts. Here is what the first few pages highlight about why India is currently seeing steady growth.

1. The "7.4%" Growth Projection

The first big data point is that India’s real GDP growth is estimated at 7.4%. The Survey points out that while the global environment is quite "uncertain" right now, India’s momentum is largely being driven by domestic demand—basically, the activity happening right here within our borders.

2. Infrastructure as a Driver

There’s a significant focus on construction, which also grew by 7.4%. The report attributes this to sustained public capital expenditure—which is the official way of saying the focus remains on building roads, bridges, and digital systems. The Survey describes this as a "multiplier effect" that supports other industries like steel and cement.

3. Industrial Resilience

The word "resilient" appears quite a bit. The data shows that even with disruptions like heavy rainfall affecting mining, manufacturing has stayed on track. According to the high-frequency indicators (like the PMI), the demand for "Made in India" goods remains robust.

4. A Forward-Looking View

The Survey doesn't just look back; it uses "nowcasting" to predict near-term trends. It suggests that with things like GST rationalization and a favorable policy environment, industrial activity is expected to remain buoyant heading into the next quarter.

My Takeaway So Far: The main theme of the opening chapter is "Strategic Resilience." It suggests that by focusing on internal strengths—like our own shoppers and builders—the economy is creating a buffer against global trade disruptions.

What’s coming in Part 2? I’ll be looking into what the Survey says about the "External Sector" (our trade with other countries) and the interesting sections on employment trends.

Are you finding the economy easier to understand when we stick to the core facts? Let me know by sharing it with anyone who wants to stay informed!
