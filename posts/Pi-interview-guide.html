Preparing for IIM PI Interviews: What I’ve Learned So Far

Feb 2, 2026 · 8 min read

Hey everyone, today I am going to share how you can prepare for a PI interview.

Wait—why would you listen to me? What experience do I really have? I have zero full-time work experience and have only given a few interviews during placements. When it comes to MBA interviews, I am as new as you can imagine.

However, based on my past corporate interview experiences and guidance from seniors at IIMs, I have been preparing seriously for PI interviews. I am simply sharing my learnings, which might help you as well.

Today, I will cover some important HR questions that are commonly asked in IIM interviews—although the sky is the limit.

1. Tell Me About Yourself

This is the most important question and almost always asked. If 10 interviews are conducted, there is a very high chance that this question will come up in 9 of them.

Although it seems easy, this question decides the flow of your entire interview. Structuring it properly is extremely important.

A good structure is: academics → projects / positions of responsibility / certifications → a brief hint of why you decided to pursue an MBA (do not explain it in detail here).

Sample Answer (Fresher – Engineer)

Good morning, and thank you for the opportunity. My name is Deepayan Das.

I come from a technical background. I completed my Diploma in Electronics and Telecommunication Engineering from XYZ Polytechnic, followed by a B.Tech in Electronics and Communication Engineering from XYZ Institute. These experiences gave me a strong practical foundation and a disciplined problem-solving approach.

During my engineering, I realized that I perform best in challenging situations. A key example was my final-year project on a 15-volt current sense circuit. Just before submission, one of our core team members met with an accident. I had to quickly assess the situation, redistribute responsibilities, and take ownership of critical tasks to ensure we delivered a working prototype within the deadline. This experience taught me leadership, accountability, and decision-making under pressure.

Although I enjoy technical work and validated my aptitude by clearing GATE, I gradually understood that my long-term interest lies in managing people, resources, and decisions rather than working only at a technical level. I want to build the skills required to solve larger, real-world problems and create meaningful impact.

Outside academics, I enjoy long evening walks and playing video games. These activities help me stay calm, focused, and mentally balanced, which I believe are important qualities for leadership roles.

Thank you.

Note: Don’t copy hobbies or positions of responsibility unless you have genuinely done them. Saying anything false can lead to traps and grilling.

2. Why MBA?

I am not providing a sample answer here because everyone’s background, goals, and aspirations are different.

Your answer should clearly cover: where you are currently, what skills you have, what skills you lack, where you want to see yourself in the future, and how an MBA will help you bridge that gap.

3. Gap Year Question

If you have a gap year, this question will almost certainly come up. Do not highlight it yourself, but if the panel asks, do not deny it. Explain it honestly and logically.

Sample Answer (Fresher – Engineer)

After completing my B.Tech, I wanted to make a well-informed decision about my long-term career rather than rushing into the next step. I prepared for and appeared in GATE to objectively assess my technical strength and career inclination.

The experience helped me realize that while I can handle technical problem-solving, a purely technical or research-intensive career is not where I see myself creating long-term impact.

During this phase, I explored management as a career option and realized that decision-making, coordination, and accountability aligned more closely with my interests. Due to a broader hiring slowdown, I could not secure a role that matched my learning goals, so I used this time to gain clarity and prepare seriously for CAT.

My gap year was a conscious transition phase that helped me make a deliberate shift toward management.

Note: Don’t copy-paste unless it genuinely matches your journey.

4. Strengths and Weaknesses

At this stage, you should be aware of your strengths and weaknesses. Mention at least two strengths and clearly explain how you are working to improve your weaknesses.

5. If Not Selected Here, Then What?

Even if you have other calls, avoid saying you will simply join another college. Show why this institute matters to you.

Sample Answer

XYZ is my clear first preference, and I have prepared specifically with this institute in mind. I am not looking for just an MBA tag but the right environment to grow, and for me, that environment is XYZ.

6. Tell Me About Your Hometown

Keep it brief: city or village, state, and a short economic or cultural context. If the panel wants more, they will ask.

7. Tell Me Something Not in Your Resume

You can share a habit, challenge, or mindset that shaped you.

One thing not explicitly mentioned in my resume is how intentionally I work on self-discipline and consistency. I focus on building daily systems rather than relying on motivation, which helps me stay composed under pressure.

Blind copying can be as fatal as Chatur’s speech in 3 Idiots. Understanding your own story and sharing it honestly will always be a boon.

That’s all for today. Do share this with your friends who have their PI interviews coming up.
